Research Pillars

Three Pillars of Hematologic Discovery

Our research program is structured around three interconnected themes ‚Äî each grounded in clinical need and designed to generate findings that can change how patients are diagnosed and treated.

01

Early Identification of Rare Hematologic Disease

Many rare blood disorders ‚Äî including paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH), immune thrombocytopenia (ITP), and aplastic anemia ‚Äî are frequently missed or delayed in diagnosis. The consequences for patients can be severe: disease progression, thrombotic events, and treatment delays that affect long-term outcomes.

Our work focuses on identifying the subtle laboratory signals present early in disease course. By studying large longitudinal datasets, we aim to define patterns that can alert clinicians to investigate further ‚Äî before the disease declares itself more dramatically. This includes work on novel screening pathways, diagnostic algorithms, and clinical decision rules.

02

Biomarkers & AI-Driven Clinical Decision Making

The clinical laboratory generates enormous amounts of data ‚Äî complete blood counts, flow cytometry panels, morphology reports ‚Äî that go largely underutilized in their predictive capacity. We are developing machine learning models that learn from this data to support real-world clinical decision-making.

Our AI research focuses on building and validating predictive models using institutional longitudinal data. These models are designed not as black boxes, but as explainable tools that can be interpreted and trusted by clinicians. Key goals include risk stratification for disease progression, prediction of treatment response, and flagging of atypical laboratory patterns warranting specialist review.

03

Clonal Evolution in PNH & Bone Marrow Failure

Paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria is a clonal disorder of hematopoietic stem cells, characterized by the expansion of PNH clones that escape complement-mediated destruction. The relationship between clone size, disease trajectory, and clinical outcomes remains incompletely understood.

We are building and analyzing longitudinal cohorts of PNH patients to understand how clonal evolution over time shapes the clinical phenotype. This work links serial flow cytometry data, complement activation markers, and clinical events to build a more complete picture of disease natural history ‚Äî with the goal of informing monitoring strategies and treatment decisions.

Why It Matters

Rare Diseases, Real Impact

Rare hematologic diseases are rare by definition ‚Äî but for the patients who have them, they are anything but rare. Delayed diagnosis, misclassification, and lack of evidence-based guidance create a high burden for patients and clinicians alike.

Our research aims to change the diagnostic journey for patients with rare blood disorders ‚Äî shortening the path from symptom to diagnosis, and from diagnosis to the right treatment.

By generating robust real-world evidence and making it accessible to clinicians, we believe we can meaningfully improve outcomes for a patient population that has historically been underserved by clinical research.
